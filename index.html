Vanishing Labor: A Timeline of Technological Unemployment

  • Ancient Era
  • Medieval Era
  • 1500 to 1700

Ancient Era

Technological unemployment has existed since ancient times. Early civilizations combated this by developing relief programs to support unemployed workers. Many rulers would refuse labor-saving innovations out of fear of widespread unemployment. Philosophers, such as Aristotle, questioned how the development of machines would impact human labor, stating, "When looms weave by themselves, man's slavery will end"...

1500 - 1700

From the 16th to 18th century, innovations in textiles, steam power, and metallurgy reshaped industries and accelerated job displacement. A rise in mercantalism grew, particularly in Britain, where the thought that improved technologies would allow for more employment, because Britain would be able to defend itself against foreign markets. As such, government prioritized efficiency and would not protect workers from unemployment like they had in the past.

1589 AD - The stocking frame was an early knitting machine that automated the production of stockings. Queen Elizabeth I famously refused to grant a patent to its inventor, fearing widespread unemployment. Its inventor ended up selling them in France.
